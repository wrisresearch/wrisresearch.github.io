Influence as a Professional Responsibility

Advisory professionals must also influence stakeholders. Influence is not the same as authority. In many client or internal settings, auditors and advisors do not hold direct power over decisions. Instead, influence comes from credibility, expertise, and relationship building. Stakeholders often arrive at decisions based on their own priorities, which may not align with recommended actions. In such cases, advisors must communicate with clarity and honesty. Gentle persuasion through direct, thoughtful communication can help stakeholders understand the rationale behind recommendations. People are more likely to support solutions when they feel respected and when the evidence is presented in a way that relates to their interests (Deloitte).

When persuasion alone is not sufficient, advisors may need to consider how to align interests. Influence without authority can involve exchanging insight, support, or access to resources as a way of securing cooperation. In professional services settings, this might mean offering deeper analysis, facilitating connections with subject matter experts, or providing guidance that helps others meet their objectives while advancing the proposed changes. On rare occasions, influence strategies may involve pressure through social norms or organizational expectations. For example, auditors can highlight peer performance trends to encourage compliance or adoption of best practices. In this way, advisory professionals use evidence not simply to persuade but to shift cultural attitudes toward transparency, accountability, and shared responsibility. These approaches must always be applied with care, as influence that feels coercive can undermine trust and professional relationships (Deloitte).
